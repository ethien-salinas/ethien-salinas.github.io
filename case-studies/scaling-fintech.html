Building a Fintech Platform for Migrants in Europe

How I helped take Íkualo from an early idea to an operating fintech platform, building the technology function, evolving the product, and preparing the company for scale.

Executive Summary Íkualo started as a bold idea: to build a financial platform designed for Latin American migrants in Europe. I joined at the earliest stage, first helping define the technical path and evaluate providers, and later building the internal technology function, evolving the product beyond a white-label approach, and supporting the company through its transition into a scale-up stage.

Context

Íkualo was created with the vision of becoming a financial platform specifically designed for Latin American migrants living in Europe. The mission was not only to offer access to financial services, but to do so in a way that was practical, trustworthy, and compliant from day one.

I met Harold Correa, the founder and CEO, when the company was still at the idea stage. He had already been exploring the opportunity and was looking for someone with fintech experience who could help turn the concept into a real product and a viable technology platform.

At that point, the company was extremely lean: CEO, marketing, design, PR, and me as the only technical figure. My initial contribution combined advisory work, vendor evaluation, technical translation, and execution planning.

The Challenge

The vision was compelling, but there was no clear product or technical roadmap yet. We needed to define how to build a compliant financial product in Europe, move quickly enough to validate the market, and avoid getting boxed in by short-term decisions.

  • There was no established product direction or implementation roadmap.
  • We needed to assess Banking-as-a-Service and KYC providers suitable for launch.
  • We had to balance speed to market with regulatory, security, and operational requirements.
  • The platform needed to be extensible enough to grow beyond a basic account product.

My Role

I joined as CTO and initially worked in a very hands-on strategic role: evaluating providers, translating their capabilities into architecture and product implications, and helping shape the first realistic path to market.

  • Evaluated Banking-as-a-Service and fintech infrastructure providers.
  • Created diagrams and technical explanations to support business decisions.
  • Coordinated delivery with external vendors during the first phase.
  • Built the internal engineering structure as the company evolved.
  • Worked closely on product definition, feature sequencing, and technical feasibility.

Over time, my role evolved from advisor and delivery driver into building and leading the internal technology function.

Strategy

In the early stage, the right decision was to optimize for speed to market. The first version of the product relied on a white-label fintech setup, which made it possible to launch faster and start learning from the market.

But that was never meant to be the final state. As soon as the business and product direction became clearer, the limitations of the initial provider model became evident: reduced flexibility, slower iteration, and constraints on product differentiation.

The strategy then shifted toward progressively building internal capabilities, reducing vendor dependency, and creating a more extensible platform that could support future products.

Phase 1

Launch quickly

Use external fintech infrastructure and a white-label approach to reach the market and validate the proposition.

Phase 2

Gain control

Build internal services and reduce dependency on vendor limitations that slowed product evolution.

Phase 3

Prepare for scale

Strengthen architecture, team structure, and product capabilities to support new financial services and growth.

Platform Architecture

The platform evolved toward a cloud-native microservices architecture designed for flexibility, integration, and long-term product growth.

Core stack

  • AWS cloud infrastructure
  • Node.js / NestJS backend
  • Microservices architecture
  • React Native mobile app
  • Docker and Kubernetes for containerization and orchestration

Key integrations

  • Banking-as-a-Service provider
  • KYC provider
  • SMS, email, WhatsApp, and push notifications
  • Google Wallet
  • Apple Wallet

Product Capabilities

The product moved from concept to a real customer-facing financial experience with a fully digital onboarding flow and core banking-related features.

  • 100% digital account opening flow
  • Onboarding and KYC completed online
  • Biometric registration for login
  • Spanish IBAN account
  • Ability to request a card from the start
  • Integration with Apple Wallet and Google Wallet
  • Security and compliance foundations such as SCA and password policies

Building the Engineering Organization

One of the most important parts of my work was not just building the platform, but building the function that could sustain and evolve it.

Early evolution

  • Started as the only technical role
  • Then added 3 full-stack developers
  • Later incorporated UX/UI and DevOps capabilities

Current structure

  • Architect
  • UX/UI
  • QA
  • Tech leads
  • Full-stack engineers
  • DevOps (external)

Company Growth

The broader company also evolved significantly from the initial five-person setup into a much more complete organization supporting operations, customer service, marketing, and technology.

  • General management
  • Administration and finance
  • Marketing team with 3 internal people plus external agency support
  • Customer support team of 8 people covering all shifts
  • Technology team of 15 internal people plus specialized outsourced services such as security reviews

Results

  • Took the company from idea stage to an operating fintech platform.
  • Launched a product serving thousands of users.
  • Built the internal technology area from scratch.
  • Created the technical foundation to expand beyond the initial account product.
  • Helped support the transition from startup execution to scale-up readiness.

Difficult Decisions

Some of the hardest decisions were organizational rather than purely technical.

As the company evolved, it became clear that some team members who had shown promise in the early stage were no longer the right fit for the next phase. Startup dynamics change quickly, and not everyone adapts equally well to increasing complexity, higher expectations, and a more demanding pace.

Another important shift was moving away from relying too heavily on junior profiles. At first, hiring junior talent felt like a financially responsible choice. In practice, it often created hidden costs: slower execution, weaker systems thinking, more basic mistakes, and increased risk in a regulated environment.

Over time, I adjusted the hiring strategy toward stronger seniority, which improved quality, speed, ownership, and the team’s ability to understand the system as a whole rather than only individual features.

Key Learnings

Building a fintech company is a multi-stage journey. Going from idea to product, from product to validation, and from validation to scale each requires different decisions, different team profiles, and different leadership styles.

In fintech, security and regulation are often invisible when everything goes well. But they are always there, and they matter deeply. Very few people will mention them proactively, yet everyone will expect them to be fully under control the moment anything goes wrong.

I also learned that team design must evolve with the company. What works in a five-person startup will not necessarily work in a growing organization with broader operational, customer, and regulatory responsibilities.

CTO Perspective

Building a fintech platform is not only a technology challenge. It is an organizational challenge, a product challenge, and a sequencing challenge. The real work is making architecture, people, and business priorities evolve together.

— Ethien Salinas
